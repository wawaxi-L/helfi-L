## Player 2

The rules are simple:
1. Listen to what the other person has to say.
2. If you don’t understand something, ask for clarification. Don’t question the other person unless you’re absolutely certain.
3. Listen actively (but not confrontationally)—so let the other person know from time to time that you’re still listening, including by asking follow-up questions. This isn’t about what the other person thinks, but how they feel. 
4. Under no circumstances should you question the other person’s memory during this session.
5. Don’t speculate too much. This could reinforce the other person’s situation. It’s okay to discuss things, but never present an opinion as a fact.
6. Each person can only take on the role of Helfi once every two days. This is to prevent you from becoming the other person’s personal counselor. You also need to understand that you don’t HAVE to help, but you MAY if you WANT to.
7. Finally, you should point out that the person is also welcome to speak with other family members, a pastor, or a therapist for pastoral care. This, too, helps prevent you from becoming the person’s personal counselor.
The source for rules 1–5 is my own experience as someone who sought help and encountered misunderstandings.

# Step

## Player 1

Talk to the other person about your concerns. Don’t mark this step as complete until you feel that your Helfi has been heard.
If you feel misunderstood:
1. Take a moment to breathe before explaining everything again.
2. If that’s not enough, ask, “How did you understand what I just said?”
3. Listen. Really listen. Don’t judge. Don’t jump in to correct them right away.
4. Correct them politely: If something was misunderstood, explain it again—keeping in mind how the other person thinks.
The source for these tips is [Kerstin Wemheuer’s blog](https://www.wemheuer.de/blog/warum-du-staendig-missverstanden-wirst)
